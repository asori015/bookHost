For Further Reading

BIOGRAPHY AND BACKGROUND

Beginning with Elizabeth Gaskell’s Life of Charlotte Brontë (1857), innumerable accounts of the lives and the world of the Brontës have been published—so many that the family has acquired legendary status. Myths about the Brontës have also circulated widely, and much recent scholarship has been devoted to sorting fact from fiction. The two biographies noted below are even-handed studies, while the collection of letters enables the Brontës to speak in their own voices. Emily’s poetry, which stands on its own merit, can also be read in relation to Wuthering Heights. Although details concerning the inception and the production of the novel are scant, several accounts have been written, including the one cited below.
 

Barker, Juliet. The Brontës. London: Weidenfeld and Nicolson, 1994. A comprehensive account of the closely intertwined family.
———, ed. The Brontës: A Life in Letters. New York: Overlook Press, 1998. An extensive selection of correspondence that is dominated by Charlotte but that nevertheless represents the elusive Emily through letters and diary papers.
Chitham, Edward. The Birth of Wuthering Heights: Emily Brontë at Work. New York: St. Martin’s Press, 1986. An interesting account of the creation of the novel.
Gaskell, Elizabeth. The Life of Charlotte Brontë. 1857. Reprint: New York: Penguin, 1998.
Gérin, Winifred. Emily Brontë: Biography. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1971. A strong modern biography.
Gezari, Janet, ed. Emily Brontë: The Complete Poems. Har mondsworth, UK: Penguin, 1992. An accessible edition of the poetry.

CRITICISM AND GUIDES

From its initial publication, Wuthering Heights has provoked strong reactions from readers, and controversy over the novel has not abated. Lending itself to a variety of critical perspectives and interpretations, Wuthering Heights continues to be one of the most widely discussed nineteenth-century British texts. While readers can benefit from the many study guides that have been produced (one is noted below), essay collections provide a good initiation into the abundance of commentary on Wuthering Heights as well as offering a sampling of approaches to the novel itself.
 

Bloom, Harold, ed. Emily Brontë’s Wuthering Heights. New York: Chelsea House, 1987. This collection includes a variety of modern critical interpretations.
Knoepfelmacher, U. C. Wuthering Heights: A Study. Landmarks of World Literature. Athens: Ohio University Press, 1994. One of many helpful guides, this one offers detailed insights into the novel.
Miles, Peter. Wuthering Heights: The Critics Debate. Houndmills, Baskingstoke, Hampshire, UK: Macmillan, 1990. This is an introductory collection.
Stoneman, Patsy, ed. Emily Brontë: Wuthering Heights. Columbia Critical Guides. New York: Columbia University Press, 1998. With its broad array of nineteenth- and twentieth-century views, as well as a comprehensive bibliography, this compendium is a good starting point for becoming acquainted with the many and varied responses to the novel.
Vogler, Thomas A., ed. Twentieth Century Interpretations of Wuthering Heights: A Collection of Critical Essays. Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice-Hall, 1968. This anthology brings together many important older critical essays and includes responses to the novel by such writers as Virginia Woolf and V. S. Pritchett.
